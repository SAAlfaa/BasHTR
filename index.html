BASHtr · Benchmark for Arabic-script Handwritten Text Recognition

Transcription Guidelines

Conventions for producing training data for generalized Automatic Text Recognition of Arabic-script manuscripts. One graphemic standard for any manuscript, in any language written in Arabic script.

بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم مسئلة في الموسيقى لثابت بن قره

¶§ 1

Introduction

These guidelines define transcription conventions for the production of training data for generalized Automatic Text Recognition (ATR) of Arabic-script manuscripts. They are developed within the BASHtr project and will be refined iteratively as the project's corpus grows and community feedback is integrated.

While currently experimental, they are designed from the outset with generalizability in mind: any Arabic-script manuscript, regardless of the language it records, should be transcribable under these conventions. Arabic script has historically served Arabic, Persian, Ottoman Turkish, Urdu, Malay, Hausa, and many other languages; a single unified standard covers them all.

The rationale sections (§ 1–6) explain the reasoning behind the conventions. Transcribers who simply wish to apply them can skip ahead to the character inventory.

¶§ 2

Why ATR needs its own conventions

Transcription guidelines for ATR serve a fundamentally different purpose than those familiar from scholarly editing. An edition presents a text in a form suited to a research question; ATR guidelines must produce the data from which a machine learning model learns to convert manuscript images into text. Three constraints follow that arise only incidentally in editorial work.

Efficiency

Conventions must allow the production of large volumes of consistently annotated data.

Learnability

Rules must be learnable by statistical models that operate with limited context.

Versatility

Datasets produced by different projects must be combinable into the large, diverse corpora that generalized models require.

¶§ 3

The transcription spectrum

Following the taxonomy of Robinson and Solopova (Canterbury Tales Project), transcription standards range from graphetic (record every visual variant) through graphemic (record the functional units of the writing system) to normalized (impose conventions requiring knowledge beyond the page). These are idealized points on a continuous spectrum; any real standard combines elements of each.

Feature Graphetic BASHtr (graphemic) Normalized
Allographic variants (positional forms) Transcribed as distinct Unified to one codepoint unify Unified
Consonantal dotting (iʿjām) As seen on page As seen on page keep Corrected if missing or wrong
Vowel marks (fatḥa, kasra, ḍamma, sukūn, tanwīn) As seen Omitted omit Restored via linguistic analysis
Shadda (gemination) As seen Omitted omit Restored via linguistic analysis
Hamza As seen Precomposed codepoints, only when visible keep Normalized

A fully graphetic transcription is extremely demanding and produces a long tail of rare character classes; a normalized one requires knowledge that is not on the page and yields ambiguous image-to-text mappings prone to hallucination, especially in multilingual collections.

¶§ 4

The graphemic compromise

These guidelines adopt the graphemic level as their foundation. Experience from the Latin-script domain shows this is the optimal trade-off: the CATMuS project assembled over 160,000 graphemically transcribed lines across 12 languages, and models trained on it match those trained on a semi-diplomatic corpus nearly thirty times larger in language-specific material.

Where the guidelines deviate from strict graphemic transcription, they do so deliberately and conservatively: they remove information that is unreliable to annotate or detrimental to model training, while preserving everything a competent reader needs to work with the text.

preserved

  • The consonantal skeleton
  • Original orthography
  • Dotting exactly as present, even if scribally incorrect
  • Language-specific letters (Persian, Urdu, Jawi, Maghribī)
  • Hamza when visually present

deliberately omitted

  • All vowel marks (fatḥa, kasra, ḍamma, sukūn, tanwīn, wasla)
  • Shadda
  • Taṭwīl used for justification
  • Presentation-form and sacred-phrase ligatures
  • Punctuation and decorative symbols
¶§ 5

Benefits

Language independence

A single set of conventions covers most languages written in Arabic script. Datasets can be freely combined, allowing training of more versatile models.

Improved ATR performance

A manageable character inventory, deterministic image-to-text mappings, and no non-learnable normalization rules: models reach high accuracy with less data and generalize better.

Lower barriers

Fewer intricate rules and no counterintuitive normalizations mean more efficient data creation and higher inter-annotator agreement.

¶§ 6

A note on downstream use

A transcription produced under these guidelines will not, by itself, satisfy all humanities use cases: critical editions normalize orthography, Ottomanists transliterate, linguists may want full vocalization. These needs are best addressed as post-processing on a reliable base transcription rather than baked into the ATR stage.

Normalization applied programmatically to a correct base text is more reliable and more versatile than training a model to produce normalized output directly: it can be adapted to any scholarly tradition, applied selectively, and corrected systematically. The base transcription is produced once; the normalizations are as many as the research questions that require them.

¶§ 7

Character inventory and graphemic mapping

The character inventory is the smallest set that preserves all graphemic distinctions present in the script. When in doubt, fewer classes are better.

Use the Arabic (U+0600–U+06FF), Arabic Supplement (U+0750–U+077F), and Arabic Extended-A (U+08A0–U+08FF) blocks. Arabic Presentation Forms (U+FB50–U+FEFF) are not used. All positional variants of a letter (initial, medial, final, isolated) are transcribed as a single code point: the recognizer sees the visual form, the transcription records only the graphemic identity.

Notes on designated codepoints

Where a grapheme has more than one Unicode representation across languages, a single codepoint is designated corpus-wide:

Kāf is always ك U+0643, regardless of language. Final yāʾ is ي U+064A when dotted and ى U+0649 when dotless; initial and medial yāʾ is always ي. Hāʾ and tāʾ marbūṭa are transcribed as written: ه or ة; if tāʾ marbūṭa context is present but the dots are absent, ه is used, since missing dots are never restored. Language-specific letters use their dedicated codepoint whenever visually present; an Arabic-block letter is never substituted. Maghribī fāʾ and qāf use ڢ and ڧ only when written Maghribī-style; standard ف / ق otherwise.

¶§ 8

Diacritics

Vowel marks omit

All vowel marks (fatḥa, kasra, ḍamma, sukūn, the tanwīn series, wasla, maddah when not part of آ) are omitted.

كِتَاب → كتاب

Shadda omit

Shadda is omitted, a deliberate deviation from strict graphemic transcription: manuscript practice is too inconsistent to transcribe reliably. Gemination is recovered downstream from lexical and morphological analysis.

مُدَرِّس → مدرس

Consonantal dotting (iʿjām) preserve

Transcribed exactly as present on the page, including incorrect, eccentric, or scribally inconsistent dotting. Missing dots are never restored. Crossed-out or struck-through dotting is ignored.

If a bāʾ carries three dots, transcribe ث, even if contextually wrong.

Ambiguous dotting decision rule

1. Is the dot count clear?
NO → use the undotted skeleton form (ٮ, ٯ, ں). Do not guess.
YES → step 2.
2. Is the dot position clear?
NO → choose the most common member of the letter family.
YES → transcribe the exact dotted letter.
¶§ 9

Hamza

Precomposed carriers

Precomposed carrier + hamza codepoints are used where they exist: أ U+0623, إ U+0625, ؤ U+0624, ئ U+0626, آ U+0622.

Standalone hamza

Hamza with no visible carrier: ء U+0621.

No hamza on the page

If no hamza is present on the page, none is supplied.

Raised hamza

Persian high hamza and raised hamza above non-standard carriers: encode as base letter + U+0654 (combining hamza above) only when distinctly visible as a raised mark; otherwise treat as ordinary hamza.

¶§ 10

Numerals

Digits

Transcribe numerals using the codepoints that match Western Arabic: 0–9 (U+0030–U+0039).

Abjad numerals

Letters used as numerical values are transcribed as their letter forms; the numerical function is recovered downstream.

¶§ 11

Spacing, justification, and ligatures

Word boundaries

Follow the visible spacing of the manuscript. Typographic space U+0020 separates words. Zero-width non-joiner U+200C is not inserted; meaningful half-spaces in Persian and Urdu are handled downstream.

Taṭwīl omit

Taṭwīl U+0640 used for justification or aesthetic purposes is not transcribed.

Ligatures

Lām-alif is transcribed as ل + ا; the presentation-form ligature is not used. Sacred-phrase ligatures with dedicated codepoints (U+FDF2, U+FDFA, U+FDFB, and similar) are never used.

¶§ 12

Abbreviations, symbols, and sigla

Anything that looks like text is transcribed. The boundary is visual, not functional: if a mark is composed of recognizable letter forms or conventional signs, it enters the transcription.

Abbreviations and sigla

Abbreviations are never expanded; the abbreviation mark itself is transcribed if it has a visual presence on the page. Scribal sigla (ṣaḥḥ, naḫḫ, collation marks, numerical annotations) are transcribed as text. Diagram labels and text embedded in non-textual elements are transcribed.

Signes de renvoi pending

The convention for signes de renvoi and reference marks is still to be determined: either transcribe with a designated symbol or annotate as a region type.

¶§ 13

Decorative and non-textual elements

Punctuation, decorative symbols, and non-textual elements are not transcribed. Text embedded in decoration, diagrams, tables, or illustrations is transcribed under the same rules, segmented as its own line(s) or region(s).

¶§ 14

Deletions, corrections, and damage

Preserve as much legible text as possible. The goal is to maximize the amount of usable image-text pairs available for training, not to reconstruct the author's intended text.

Deletions and corrections

Deleted text is transcribed if legible. Interlinear and marginal corrections are transcribed as separate lines if they change the vertical extent of the line; kept inline if they do not.

Damage and erasure

At approximately word scale, the line is retained and illegible portions are skipped. Large-scale damage: the area is segmented into multiple lines around the damaged zone.

¶§ 15

Layout and segmentation

Line images fed to the recognizer should have consistent vertical extent and approximately linear progression. The most widespread line data model for manuscripts is baseline + bounding polygon. Segmentation choices that violate these properties produce training data that degrades model performance; the recommendations below are to some extent subjective, and minor deviations will not substantially affect recognition accuracy.

Above- and below-line additions

Kept with the main line if they do not significantly change its vertical extent; otherwise segmented as separate lines.

Baseline curving and heaping

A heaped element is transcribed on a separate baseline if it cannot be connected to the rest of the line with a smooth baseline, i.e. if an abrupt change of baseline direction would be necessary. Otherwise it stays part of the text line.

Marginalia

Always segmented as separate lines or regions. Reading-order conventions for multi-column, glossed, or commentary layouts are out of scope for now.
